The Overview

Pete's Lunchbox is a local food truck near Drexel University that operated solely through in-person ordering. Over 10 weeks, I worked as a UI Designer and Developer on a five-person team to turn existing static designs into a functional mobile ordering app.

My work covered user personas, the style guide, wireframes, prototyping, microinteractions, and the final HTML/CSS/JS layout. The app received positive usability feedback and performed stably throughout QA testing.

The Context and Challenge

Background / Description / Timeline / Budget / Purpose

Pete's Lunchbox is a food truck serving the Drexel community that had no digital ordering system before this project. A previous team had created initial UI/UX designs, but the app was never built out functionally. Our team of five took on the task of developing a working application within a 10-week timeline under tight time and resource constraints.

The Problem

Without a digital ordering option, Pete's Lunchbox could only reach customers who were physically present. This limited customer reach and made order management inefficient during peak hours. The app was created to address both issues by introducing mobile ordering, reducing wait times, and making the truck accessible beyond foot traffic.

Goals & Objectives

01
User Satisfaction: Achieve positive usability testing feedback and high satisfaction scores during pre-launch testing.
02
Operational Efficiency: Demonstrate reduced manual workload through streamlined ordering workflows in testing scenarios.
03
System Performance: Ensure fast load times and minimal bugs or crashes during development and QA testing.

Team & Role

I worked on a five-person team as a UI Designer and Developer. My responsibilities included user personas, the style guide, wireframes, prototyping, microinteractions, and building the final front-end layout.

The Process and Insight

01

Target Audience / User Personas

I developed three user personas to guide design decisions throughout the project. Persona 1 was a Drexel student who prioritized speed and needed to order and pick up food quickly between back-to-back classes. Persona 2 was a Drexel student who valued high-protein options, fast pickup, and clear menu information. Persona 3 was a construction worker on a short lunch break who needed a convenient, no-friction ordering experience.

02

Sketches / Wireframes

The team built on wireframes from the previous term rather than starting from scratch. I reviewed and refined these to better reflect our personas and the requirements of a functional app, focusing on areas where the user flow had gaps or unclear navigation.

03

Style Guide / Prototypes

The visual direction for the app was vintage minimalist modern. The style guide covered typography, color palette, spacing, iconography, and component states, and it was used across the team to keep the design consistent from screen to screen. The project went through both an alpha and a beta prototype. The alpha served as the first functional pass, while the beta incorporated revisions based on feedback, tightening the flow and fixing inconsistencies before the final build.

04

Microinteractions / Final Project

I designed and built three microinteractions in CodePen using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, which were later integrated into the final app: a horizontally scrollable category filter bar, an interactive tip selector that updated the order total in real time, and an ASAP pickup button that displayed an estimated ready time. I also coded the final application layout using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, translating the prototype screens into a working front end with full layout structure, style guide-based component styling, and integrated microinteractions.

The Solution

The final product is a mobile ordering app that lets customers browse the menu by category, build their order, select a tip, and choose a pickup time. The design follows the vintage minimalist modern direction established in the style guide, keeping the visual language clean and consistent throughout.

01

Category Scroll Bar

The category scroll bar simplifies menu navigation by letting users move quickly between food and drink sections.

02

Tip Selector

The tip selector makes checkout straightforward and updates the order total in real time as the user makes a selection.

03

ASAP Pickup Button

The ASAP pickup button gives users a real-time estimated wait, directly addressing what all three personas needed from the experience.

04

Consistent Visual Direction

The interface stays visually cohesive through the vintage minimalist modern style guide, from browsing through checkout.

Lessons Learned

What worked well

Iterating through multiple prototypes made a noticeable difference in the final product. Each version surfaced issues that earlier versions did not.

What I would do differently

Given more time, I would focus on making the user flow feel even smoother through better transitions and clearer interactions.

Future opportunities

Future refinement could improve the small moments in the ordering experience so the overall app feels even more polished.
